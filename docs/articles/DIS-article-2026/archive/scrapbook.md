"The goal posts keep moving." Or rather: the whole game is changing — again.

How do you build for constant change — to adapt and take advantage of it? This post is about building in an evolutionary way and keeping up with the pace of change: not building for a specific technology, but building for change itself. Change comes in different flavours — the technology, the way we work, the way we think about architecture, the way we think about business — and we need to be able to adapt to all of them.

The cornerstone of our approach has been "evolutionary architecture": modularity, loose coupling, and clear interfaces. When we built our data platform during the 'big data' push, separation of compute and storage was a key principle that still serves us well today. It allowed us to evolve our compute layer independently of our storage layer and to take advantage of new technologies as they emerged. Our early adoption of Kubernetes and Spark also paid off, giving us a platform that could scale with our needs and support a wide range of use cases — from analytics and visualization to machine learning and deep learning.

The shift from classical analytics to machine learning and deep learning was a natural evolution, as we saw the potential to unlock new insights and drive new business opportunities. We started building for AI (ML → DL → GenAI) thinking the old way: building capabilities for others to use. We still are, and it is paying off. But AI 'sneaked in' while we were building — we increasingly started using it to get there ourselves. Teaching our users to accelerate, we learned even more. Context focus, skills — you name it. Developers became architects. Business and architecture merged into aligning on context, as they always did, but now for architecting with AI-assisted coding.

Along the way, every wave brought new possibilities: the beginning of generative AI, the explosion around GenAI that reached business, agents, MCP, and everything around it. AI4IT — eat your own dog food, or drink your own champagne as our Danish lead puts it — across Network, B2C, B2B, B2O, and beyond.

Meanwhile, our AI uplift, hackathons, and individual initiatives (visualization tools, experiments on databases) reshaped our daily way of working — from chatting in GitHub Copilot to agent mode with increasingly capable models, and now to context engineering full on. Skills. Claude Code. And all in a controlled, Telia way — with our LLM gateway keeping things in check.

But, we want more — sovereign AI as a service, at the edge.

Some data will never travel to the outside world. It doesn't have, and never will have, a passport to leave. Used to be a problem. Less so today. Maybe not a problem at all.

As cloud evolved, the gap between public and private capabilities expanded — initially IaaS, then PaaS, later SaaS. But open source didn’t stand still. In a way, it quietly kept doing what it has always done: Spark, Kubernetes, and now open-weight LLMs. Ideas and solutions raining from the clouds, and open source catching up on the ground.

Our private cloud is doing well. Over 10+ years, moving from vendor solutions to our own Kubernetes-based lakehouse has paid off. The concept of software-defined data centres on K8s, virtual storage, and everything on top — getting the same experience as in public cloud. That is the definition of serverless. Earlier the motivation was cost; now it is sovereignty.

EU initiatives around open ecosystems and sovereign AI platforms are accelerating this. The Arbetsförmedlingen case shows what sovereign AI looks like in practice: completely air-gapped, models hosted on-prem, and what you can do with open source now on all hardware — including AI-assisted coding, LLMs, and agent capabilities. With LiteLLM, you can use Claude Sonnet 4.5 without an Anthropic subscription. We tested it — Llama, Sonnet 4.5, the tooling is built for it.

Agentic workforces (Hinode and others within the company) are the next frontier. The skills barrier is lower than ever with AI-assisted coding closing the gap.

What about all the 'old stuff'? We are coming for it — codebases, facades to legacy systems, and more.
